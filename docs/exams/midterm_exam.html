Scenario 1: Biological Waste Treatment

Setup

A coastal wastewater treatment facility processes industrial effluent from a food-processing operation before it is discharged into a nearby estuary. Untreated or inadequately treated effluent can cause algal blooms and long-term damage to estuarine biodiversity. A team of environmental engineers wants to develop an automated workflow that flags batches of treated water as Compliant or Non-compliant with regulatory discharge standards before they are released to the environment.

The team has access to measurements from 500 treated water batches. For each batch, the following variables were recorded:

  • BOD reduction efficiency (%): percentage reduction in biological oxygen demand achieved by the treatment process
  • pH level (standard units): acidity or alkalinity of the treated effluent
  • Dissolved oxygen (DO) concentration (mg/L): amount of oxygen dissolved in the treated water
  • Microbial load (log₁₀ CFU/mL): log-transformed bacterial colony-forming unit count
  • Discharge status: Compliant or Non-compliant with regulatory standards

Question 1 Identify the predictor variables in this scenario.

Question 2 Identify the response variable in this scenario.

Question 3 Is this a regression or classification task? Justify your answer.

Question 4 Is inference or prediction the primary goal here? Justify your answer.

Question 5 Suggest one parametric and one non-parametric method that could be applied to solve this task. Clearly indicate which is which.

Non-compliant batches are rare: only about 5% of batches in the historical dataset fail standards. The engineering team’s priority is to intercept every non-compliant batch before it is released, even if that means occasionally flagging a compliant batch for re-inspection.

Scenario 2 — Forest Sustainability Management

A state forestry agency monitors 200 forest stands across a protected watershed in northern California. Land managers want to identify stands that are ecologically at risk so that limited management resources can be directed where they are most needed. A team of forest ecologists has decided to use logistic regression to classify each stand as Sustainable or At Risk.

B. Available data. For each forest stand, the team recorded:

  • Canopy cover (%): proportion of ground area covered by tree crowns
  • Basal area (m²/ha): cross-sectional area of all trees at breast height, per hectare
  • Soil moisture deficit index: composite index reflecting deviation from expected soil moisture (higher values indicate drier conditions)
  • Years since last disturbance: years elapsed since the last recorded fire, harvest, or major storm event
  • Ecological status (response): Sustainable or At Risk

C. Model. The team fits a logistic regression model to classify stands into At Risk (positive class) and Sustainable (negative class). At the end of model development, they evaluate the model on a held-out test set using a classification threshold of \(\lambda = 0.5\). The confusion matrix and decision boundary are shown below.

Confusion matrix (\(\lambda = 0.5\), 100 test observations):

Predicted: At Risk Predicted: Sustainable
True: At Risk 35 15
True: Sustainable 10 40

Question 1 (SG: 6.1, 6.2) Calculate the true positive rate (TPR) from the confusion matrix above. Then explain in plain language what this value means in the context of this problem — use the names of the classes (At Risk and Sustainable), not just “positive” and “negative.”

D. Adjusting the classification threshold. The team is considering adjusting the classification threshold to improve model performance. One team member states:

“As the classification threshold \(\lambda\) gets closer to 0, the true positive rate will decrease.”

Question 2 (SG: 6.1) Is this statement true or false? Explain your reasoning.

E. Data leakage. To prepare the data for modeling, the team follows this workflow:

  1. Collect all 200 observations.
  2. Standardize all four predictor variables using the mean and standard deviation computed from all 200 observations.
  3. Randomly split the standardized data into a training set (70%) and a test set (30%).
  4. Fit the logistic regression model on the training set.
  5. Evaluate model performance on the test set.

Question 3 (SG: 10) Identify the data leakage occurring in this workflow. What is the problem with this approach, and how should it be corrected?

Scenario 3 — Regression: Pollution Risk Management

Setup

A. Environmental context. An environmental research group is mapping fine particulate matter pollution (PM₂.₅) across a network of 300 urban and peri-urban air quality monitoring stations in Southern California. High PM₂.₅ concentrations are linked to respiratory and cardiovascular disease. The team wants to build a predictive model to estimate PM₂.₅ concentrations from meteorological and land-use data, enabling early identification of high-risk areas before costly field sampling campaigns are deployed.

B. Available data. For each monitoring station, the team recorded:

  • Industrial land-use index (0–100): composite score reflecting the intensity of nearby industrial activity
  • Wind speed (m/s): average daily wind speed at the station
  • Temperature inversion index (°C/100 m): atmospheric temperature gradient; higher values indicate stronger inversions that trap pollutants near the ground
  • Distance to nearest major roadway (km): distance to the closest highway or arterial road
  • PM₂.₅ concentration (μg/m³, response): measured 24-hour average particulate matter concentration

C. Task. The team’s primary goal is to accurately predict PM₂.₅ concentrations at locations where direct measurements are unavailable, so resources can be allocated to areas most likely to exceed air quality thresholds.

Question 1 (SG: 1.6) Is this a supervised or unsupervised learning task? Justify your answer.

Question 2 (SG: 3) Suggest two accuracy metrics that are appropriate for evaluating this model’s performance. For each, briefly explain what the metric measures.

D. Comparing models. The team divides into three independent development groups, each building a different model using the same training data. After training, they compare models using training and test MSE. The results are shown below:

Model Approach Training MSE Test MSE
Team A KNN (\(k = 3\)) 12.4 58.7
Team B Simple linear regression 38.2 41.5
Team C KNN (\(k = 20\)) 25.8 29.3

Question 3 (SG: 8) Which model is overfitting? Explain your reasoning using the values in the table.

E. Interpreting model bias and variance. A team member reviews the results in the table above and states:

“Team A’s model has a much lower training MSE than Team B’s model. This means Team A’s model must have lower variance.”

Question 4 (SG: 9) Is this statement correct? Explain why or why not, using the concepts of model bias and variance.

F. Models with different numbers of predictors. The team is now considering adding more predictor variables to Team B’s linear regression model. They plan to compare models with one, two, three, and four predictors using \(R^2\) to select the best model.

Question 5 (SG: 4) A colleague argues that \(R^2\) alone is not the right metric for comparing models with different numbers of predictors. Do you agree? What performance metric would you suggest instead, and why?

Scenario 4 — Regression: Population Ecology

Setup

A. Environmental context. Wildlife biologists at a national park are studying the factors that drive mule deer (Odocoileus hemionus) population density across the landscape. Understanding these relationships is important for informing grazing management, predator reintroduction programs, and habitat conservation planning. The team has collected annual survey data from 80 monitoring plots distributed across the park.

B. Available data. For each monitoring plot, the team recorded:

  • Forage availability index (\(X_1\), 0–10): a composite score reflecting the quantity and quality of vegetation available to deer
  • Predator encounter rate (\(X_2\), encounters/km²/month): average frequency of mountain lion sightings and track detections per unit area per month
  • Habitat patch area (\(X_3\), ha): area of the continuous forest patch in which the monitoring plot is located
  • Deer population density (\(Y\), individuals/km²): number of deer observed per square kilometer during the annual survey

The first few and last rows of the dataset are shown below:

Observation Forage Index (\(X_1\)) Predator Rate (\(X_2\)) Habitat Area (\(X_3\)) Deer Density (\(Y\))
1 7.2 0.8 145 12.4
2 4.1 2.3 67 6.1
3 6.5 1.5 203 9.7
\(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
80 5.9 1.1 189 10.2

Question 1 (SG: 1.3) Using observations 1 and 2 from the table above, express them in the \((x_i, y_i)\) notation for multiple predictors introduced in class. Write out \(x_1\), \(y_1\), \(x_2\), and \(y_2\) explicitly.

C. Multiple linear regression model. The team fits the following model:

\[Y \approx \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \beta_3 X_3\]

Question 2 (SG: 13.1) Interpret each of the four coefficient estimates (\(\hat{\beta}_0\), \(\hat{\beta}_1\), \(\hat{\beta}_2\), \(\hat{\beta}_3\)) in the context of this study. For each, explain what it represents and what its sign tells us about the relationship between that predictor and deer density.

Question 3 (SG: 13.2) Briefly explain the general process by which the coefficient estimates \(\hat{\beta}_0, \hat{\beta}_1, \hat{\beta}_2, \hat{\beta}_3\) are obtained from the data.

Question 4 (SG: 13.3) A biologist on the team suggests that instead of fitting a single multiple linear regression, the team should fit three separate simple linear regressions — one for each predictor.
